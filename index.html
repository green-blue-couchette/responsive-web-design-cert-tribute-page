George Müller

Cared for 10,024 orphans without asking for money

George Müller, a contemporary of Charles Dickens, was all too familiar with the sights of orphan children trying to survive alone in the streets of England. He was a young man living a lifestyle of gambling, theft and promiscuity, turned Christian evangelist after a radical experience during a prayer meeting, who decided he wanted to make a change. During his lifetime, he cared for over 10,000 orphans. His legacy lasts to this day.

The success of his efforts is credited to an approach that many would deem to be a most unconventional one — the conscious refrain from any form of fund-raising, and the faith that God will provide.

A timeline of George Müller's life:

  • 1805 – Müller is born in Kroppenstedt, Kingdom of Prussia.
    (Now Saxony-Anhalt, Germany.)
  • Ca. 1815 and onwards – Early years are marked by a lifestyle of theft, gambling and chasing women.
  • 1825 – Müller attends a prayer meeting together with a friend from university, where he senses an atmosphere of love that is different from the upbringing of his strict, religious father. He is moved and undergoes a change of attitude.

    Müller rejects his father's plans of becoming a clergyman, together with any funding that would have been provided by him. Müller decides to trust in divine provision from this point on.
  • 1829 – Offers to work for London Society for Promoting Christianity Amongst the Jews.
  • 1830 – Breaks ties with the London Society, desiring freedom to preach wherever he felt God leading him.
  • 1830 – Marries Mary Groves.
  • 1832 – Starts working at Bethesda Chapel in Bristol, together with his friend Henry Craik.
  • 1834 – Founds the Scriptural Knowledge Institution for Home and Abroad, together with his friend Henry Craik.
  • 1836 – Müller's work with orphans begins in the couple's own home, eventually expanding to four houses hosting a total of 130 children.
  • 1849 – Growth continues; a new home opens at Ashley Down, Bristol, with housing capacity for 300 children, with the purpose of consolidating all orphan children to one place. The architect offered to design the plans of the new house for free.
  • 1870 – 1,722 children are accommodated in five homes.
  • 1870 – Müller's wife, Mary, passes away and is deeply missed by Müller and the staff in the orphanage.
  • 1871 – Marries Susannah Grace Sanger.
  • 1875 – Müller and his wife, Susannah, embark on a 17-year-long mission trip testifying of his experiences of divine provision and encouraging others to take the same leap of faith. Their itinerary included the USA, Canada, various European countries, Egypt, Palestine, India, and many other places.
  • 1898 – Müller passes away in Bristol, shortly after waking up one morning.

Notable details about the ministry of George Müller:

  • Müller never requested money from people for the needs of his ministry, but relied on prayer to God for supplying for these needs.
  • Many times, Müller received unsolicited donations just in time before they were needed to feed the orphans he was housing.
  • Müller meticulously documented the unsolicited donations received for his ministry. The documentation was made publicly available for scrutiny.
  • Müller's efforts to educate orphan boys and girls, in trade skills that prepared them for adult life, even led to shortage of staff in factories and mines nearby the Orphan House.
  • Charles Dickens once paid a surprise visit to the orphan houses of Müller, after hearing false rumors of starving children. Dickens was so impressed by how well the children were treated, that he wrote it up for the local papers.
